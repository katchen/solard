In today’s world, almost everyone uses lithium batteries. They are rechargeable, have one of the best energy densities, and no memory effect*. But with today’s gadgets becoming smaller, containing a variety of surfaces, and loops or bends, designers are having more and more trouble placing the batteries in a way that won’t interfere with the workings of the gadget. The major flaw of lithium batteries is that they contain a block of inflexible, non-resizable matter that holds the electrons that make the battery to work. That’s where the spray-on battery paint comes in. The spray-on battery paint developed by a team of Rice University researchers in Houston, Texas overcomes the major problem of batteries for designers designing small, irregularly surfaced gadgets. With the spray-on battery technology, you could have any size battery on a curved surface, rough surface or on different mediums such as tile, plastics, glass, metal or ceramics. In addition, spraying on a battery, which takes less than a minute, is much faster than building a conventional battery that can take more than a hundred times longer to make.

What makes up the spray-on battery paint?

Just like all batteries, the battery paint consists of a polymer separator between two current collectors; a cathode*and an anode*. One version of paint medium researchers used is lithium cobalt oxide for the cathode, lithium titanium oxide for the anode, and conductive single-walled nanotubes for the polymer separator. These layers are sprayed on top of each other with a conventional airbrush. Then they are heat-sealed to make them insensitive to moisture and to keep from pealing after time. After that, multiple spray-on batteries can be paired up with a solar battery to recharge them when needed.

How is this technology currently being improved?

Currently, painting the layers of the battery needs to be done in a dry, oxygen-free room. The team of Rice University researchers is looking into a way to make the battery in normal circumstances, so it can be used by anyone, anywhere.

For a full breakdown of the team’s study, visit June 28th’s edition of Nature Scientific Reports. * Memory effect means the battery loses a little bit of its maximum energy carrying capacity after each use *a cathode is the positively charged end of a battery *an anode is the negatively charged end of a battery
